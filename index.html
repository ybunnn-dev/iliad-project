Poetics

For Aristotle, poetry was not mere ornament but mimesis or an imitation of human action that carries universal truths. Among the forms, tragedy and epic are the highest achievements.

Both aim to depict noble actions, although to him, tragedy is more concentrated and powerful while epic poetry unfolds across vast time and space. Tragedy compresses events into a light and dramatic unity that strikes the audience with greater immediacy.

“Epic poetry and Tragedy, Comedy also and Dithyrambic poetry, and the music of the flute and of the lyre in most of their forms, are all in their general conception modes of imitation. They differ, however, from one another in three respects- the medium, the objects, the manner or mode of imitation, being in each case distinct.”

— Aristotle, Poetics

Medium

Lofty verse (dactylic hexameter).

Objects

Show noble deeds and great people.

Mode

Narrative (Homer tells the story, but also lets characters deliver long speeches).

“Epic poetry agrees with Tragedy in so far as it is an imitation in verse of characters of a higher type. They differ in that Epic poetry admits but one kind of meter and is narrative in form. They differ, again, in their length: for Tragedy endeavors, as far as possible, to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun, or but slightly to exceed this limit, whereas the Epic action has no limits of time.”

— Aristotle, Poetics

Elements of Poetry

1. Plot (Mythos)

To Aristotle, the plot is the heart of a poetry. To have a good plot, a poetry must have a clear beginning, middle, and end. The events a poetry should follow logically or by necessity. A great plot includes:


a. Peripeteia (Reversal)
Turning point where fortune changes. This often transitions from good to bad.
b. Anagnorisis (Recognition)
A shift from ignorance to knowledge which often reveals hidden truths.
c. Catharsis
Here, the audience experiences pity and fear after witnessing the downfall of the characters. Thus, the emotions are then purged that leaves the audience with a sense of relief and emotional renewal.

2. Character (Ethos)

These are the figures who enact the plot. Their choices and flaws give rise to action. Characters must be consistent, appropriate, and believable. These qualities will represent the character's moral qualities through their decisions.

3. Thought (Dianoia)

These are often the themes, insights, and reasoning that are expressed in the play. Thought makes the tragedy intellectually meaningful and not just emotionally moving.

4. Diction (Lexis)

This is the style of speech and word choice. A good diction makes thought and plot more vivid, memorable, and beautiful.

5. Song (Melos)

The musical element which is often the rhythm, harmony, and choral odes. Music enriches the emotional texture of the play.

6. Spectacle (Opsis)

These are the visual staging like costumes, scenery, effects. Aristotle however considered this as the least essential, since it depends on stagecraft more than on the poet’s art.

The Epic Poem

Epic poems are long narrative verses that recount the heroic deeds and adventures of distinguished characters, often encompassing an entire civilization's beliefs, history, and mythology. They serve as foundational texts for ancient cultures.

Key Characteristics

1

Invocation of a Muse

The poet begins by appealing to a goddess (Muse) for divine inspiration to tell the story correctly.

2

In Medias Res

Latin for "into the middle of things." The narrative begins at a critical point, not at the very beginning of the history.

3

The Epic Hero

Features a hero of legendary importance who embodies the highest values and virtues of his culture.

4

Vast Setting

The action covers wide geographical areas, sometimes spanning the cosmos, the underworld, or the entire known world.

5

Statement of Theme

The poem opens with a brief introduction of the subject matter or central conflict (e.g., "Sing, Goddess, of the Rage...").

6

Epithets

Use of repeated descriptive phrases to identify characters (e.g., "swift-footed Achilles," "grey-eyed Athena").

7

Divine Intervention

Gods and goddesses play an active, often interfering, role in the hero's journey, influencing the outcome of battles.

The Iliad

Written by the blind poet Homer around the 8th century BC, The Iliad is one of the oldest extant works of Western literature. It is set during the final year of the ten-year siege of Troy (Ilium) by a coalition of Greek states.


While the war serves as the backdrop, the poem is not merely a chronicle of battle. Its primary focus is the Rage of Achilles (Menin). It explores how the anger of a single man shapes the fate of armies, exploring themes of glory (Kleos), fate, and the tragic cost of war.

The Plot Timeline

1. The Capture

Nine years after the start of the Trojan War, a town allied with Troy, Chryse, fell to the Achaeans and captured two maidens, Chryseis and Briseis. Agamemnon claimed Chryseis, while Achilles took Briseis.

2. The Plague & The Insult

Chryseis’s father, a priest of Apollo, offered a ransom. When Agamemnon refused, Apollo sent a plague upon the Achaean camp. Forced to return Chryseis, Agamemnon spitefully took Achilles’ prize, Briseis, as compensation.

3. Achilles Withdraws

Insulted, Achilles refused to fight and prayed to his mother, Thetis, to have Zeus favor the Trojans. Without their greatest warrior, the Achaeans suffered massive losses and were pushed back.

4. The Death of Patroclus

With Achilles gone, his beloved friend Patroclus stepped in to lead the Achaeans using Achilles' armor. He pushed the Trojans back but was slain by Hector (aided by Apollo).

5. The Rage Returns

Grieving and fueled by rage, Achilles reconciled with Agamemnon. Wearing new armor from Hephaestus, he returned to battle, slaughtering every Trojan he encountered.

6. The Fall of Hector

Hector was chased around the city three times until Athena tricked him into confronting Achilles. Achilles killed him, tied his body to a chariot, and dragged him for 9 days around Patroclus’ tomb.

7. The Ransom & Truce

King Priam, guided by Hermes, entered the Greek camp to beg for his son's body. Moved by the plea and memories of his own father, Achilles agreed. A truce was declared so Hector could be honored with a proper funeral.

Significance of the Work

The Literary Canon

The Iliad is widely considered the "grandfather" of Western Literature. It established the standard for the Epic genre, introducing techniques like in medias res and epithets. Its structure influenced Virgil’s Aeneid, Dante’s Inferno, and Milton’s Paradise Lost.

The Heroic Code

The poem defines the ancient Greek concept of Kleos (undying glory). It presents the tragic paradox of the hero: to gain immortality in song, one must accept an early death. It forces the audience to question the cost of pride (Hubris) and the nature of honor.

The Human Cost

Unlike a simple tale of "Good vs. Evil," Homer humanizes the enemy. The poem ends not with a battle, but with Achilles weeping alongside King Priam. It reveals that while war may bring glory, its ultimate reality is shared grief and the destruction of beautiful things.
